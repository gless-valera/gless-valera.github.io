UX Research · UI Design · Website Redesign

Endangered Species International

Redesigning a nonprofit's website to educate audiences on wildlife conservation and inspire communities to take action — from research and heuristic analysis through wireframing, usability testing, and a final high-fidelity prototype.

Endangered Species International

The Problem

A site that undermined its own mission

Endangered Species International's website required serious redesign. Common problems included heavy misuse of white space, unorganized content, and a lack of visual appeal — all of which made it harder for the organization to fulfill its core mission of inspiring people to protect wildlife.

"How might we redesign our nonprofit's website to make it so that people are inspired to take action and are well-informed on wildlife conservation projects?"

User Research

Learning from real users

Our team combined a Maze usability testing project (5 responses) with 3 in-person interviews to understand users' experiences navigating the site. We had three research objectives:

  • Can users actually understand the organization's goals and intentions?
  • What pain points and frustrations did users encounter while navigating?
  • Is donating to the organization an easy process?

After building an affinity map from interview data, four common pain points emerged:

01
The site wasn't visually pleasing — users felt it looked untrustworthy
02
Users felt suspicious of where their donations were going based on the site's content
03
The desktop layout was noticeably off — heavy misuse of white space
04
Navigation was difficult — users had to "scavenger hunt" for basic pages

"The website was not that easy to navigate because I did not even notice the top menu bar right away since it was so small and hard to see."

— Interviewee #1

"Mostly just walls of text which can be boring to read."

— Interviewee #2

Heuristic Analysis

Usability issues in detail

Beyond user feedback, we identified specific heuristic violations:

  • No visible "Donate" button: The donation function was buried under a "Support Us" tab rather than prominently displayed on the homepage.
  • Distracting sidebar: Off-topic content with inconsistent formatting cluttered the experience.
  • Inappropriate white space: Random gaps prevented pages from filling users' screens properly.
  • Outdated material: Content from over 2 years ago was prominently displayed, making the organization appear inactive.

User Journey & IA

Building a better navigation system

Using our research data, we imagined a primary user: a college student who's become interested in conservation activism and wants to learn more. This persona informed a new sitemap that streamlined the navigation bar — removing redundant pages and grouping related content together:

About Us
Consolidated organization background, values, and team info into one clear section
Latest
Fresh, current projects surfaced first — older content moved to supporting pages
Get Involved
Donation, volunteer, and community options grouped in one action-oriented hub

Wireframing & Usability Testing

Iterating toward clarity

The team created two rounds of wireframes — starting with low-fidelity sketches focused on key decisions (bigger donate button, simplified nav, multiple donation options), then building up to medium-fidelity prototypes tested through Maze with 5 participants.

Users were asked to complete three tasks: donate 00, find the organization's values, and find contact information. Heat map results confirmed that the new Donate button was large and visible enough to find easily, and that users could locate values and contact info without confusion.

Final Design

A wildlife-inspired design system

To reflect the "wildlife and nature" mission, the team used accents of brown for buttons and footers. Overly wordy buttons were replaced with icon shortcuts. The final prototype incorporated more icons, navigation breadcrumbs, proper white space usage, and a prominently placed Donate button — all validated through usability testing.

Reflection

Final thoughts

This project reinforced that allocating tasks clearly and discussing progress at stand-ups keeps design teams aligned and productive. Designing components before wireframing also allowed for much faster iteration. I'm proud of what the team delivered — and have a clear picture of what I'd improve with more time.

What's next: More usability testing on the final prototype, implementing a feature showing local conservation projects and fundraisers via location sharing.
